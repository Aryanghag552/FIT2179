The Great Barrier Reef

A Story of Coral Under Pressure

The Great Barrier Reef stretches over 2,300 km along Australia's northeast coast — the world's largest living structure. But rising ocean temperatures have triggered mass bleaching events at an alarming rate, threatening one of Earth's most vital ecosystems.

1. Australia's Reef Landscape

The Great Barrier Reef Marine Park spans three distinct regions — Northern, Central, and Southern — each with unique coral communities and varying exposure to environmental stressors. The map below shows the locations where coral bleaching has been observed and measured since the early 1990s.

Each circle represents a survey site. Size indicates the severity of bleaching observed. Data from BCO-DMO Global Coral Bleaching Database.

Bleaching severity varies significantly across the three GBR regions, with the Northern region experiencing the most severe impacts in recent events.

2. The Climate Connection

Coral bleaching is triggered when ocean temperatures rise above the threshold corals can tolerate. The relationship between sea surface temperature anomalies and bleaching events is unmistakable in the data.

Higher temperature anomalies are associated with more severe bleaching.

Bleaching peaks during the Southern Hemisphere summer months (January–March).

3. The Bleaching Crisis

Since 2016, the Great Barrier Reef has experienced six mass bleaching events — more than in the entire preceding century. The frequency and intensity of these events has accelerated dramatically, leaving less time for coral communities to recover between episodes.

Click a region in the legend to highlight it. The stacking reveals how bleaching burden shifted between regions.

Years with positive temperature anomalies (red) correspond closely with documented mass bleaching events.

4. Recovery and Outlook

Despite the devastating bleaching events, the reef has shown a remarkable capacity for recovery — when given enough time. Hard coral cover data from AIMS monitoring programs reveals cycles of decline and regrowth, but the window for recovery is narrowing as bleaching events become more frequent.

Hard coral cover percentage by GBR region from 1986 to 2025. Vertical bands indicate mass bleaching years. Data: AIMS Long-Term Monitoring Program.
